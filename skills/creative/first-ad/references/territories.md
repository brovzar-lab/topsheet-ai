## 1. Mexico

### How the 1st AD Operates in Mexico

The 1st AD in Mexico manages one of the most crew-dense production cultures in Latin America. Crews are loyal and experienced, but union obligations and permit complexity require staying ahead of the production.

### Union Obligations Affecting the Schedule

**STPC (Sindicato de Trabajadores de la Producción Cinematográfica)**
- Daily minimums: 8 hrs; overtime at hour 9 (25% premium) and hour 11 (50%)
- Mandatory rest between calls: **10 hrs minimum** (vs. 12 hr de facto standard on higher-budget shoots)
- Sunday premium: 25% surcharge on all wages — minimize Sunday shooting or budget for it explicitly
- Night premium: Scenes called after 22:00 (10 PM) trigger night differential on STPC contracts — plan accordingly
- Meal penalties: Meals must be provided within 6 hrs of crew call; penalty applies if missed

**STIC (Sindicato de Trabajadores de la Industria Cinematográfica)**
- Similar structure to STPC; some department-specific variations
- Strategic decision point is determined in pre-production (see line-producer skill)

**ANDA (Asociación Nacional de Actores)**
- Principal cast covered by ANDA agreements
- Meal and rest rules mirror STPC for scheduling purposes
- Featured extras covered under separate ANDA extras categories

### Child Actor Regulations (Mexico)
- Minors under 14: **maximum 6 hrs on set per day**, including travel
- Minors under 6: **maximum 4 hrs on set per day**
- A legal guardian must be present at all times; if parents unavailable, a DIF-designated guardian
- Night shooting (after 22:00) is prohibited for all minors under 16
- If using EFICINE/IMCINE support: comply with Instituto Mexicano de la Infancia standards

### Permit Requirements Affecting Schedule

| Permit Type | Lead Time | Impact on Schedule |
|-------------|-----------|-------------------|
| CDMX public space filming | 2–4 weeks | Block specific day on location map |
| Night shoot (CDMX) | +1–2 weeks on top of standard | Confirm before locking night exterior days |
| SEDENA (weapons and firearms) | **4–8 weeks minimum** | Start immediately in Week 1 of prep — this is a schedule-killer |
| Drone operations (AFAC) | 2–3 weeks | Block drone days early; no last-minute drone adds |
| Animal work (SEMARNAT/PROFEPA) | 2–4 weeks | Flag any animal in script at first breakdown |
| Road closures (Estado/Municipio) | 4–6 weeks | Identify all driving sequences early |
| Beach/coastline shoots (SEMARNAT) | 3–5 weeks | Ecological zones require federal approval |

### Practical Set Management in Mexico

- **Traffic & company moves in CDMX:** Factor 45–90 min for any cross-city company move during peak hours (7–10 AM, 18–21 PM)
- **Altitude (CDMX, Puebla, Querétaro):** Crew from sea level (camera operators, gaffers from other territories) may need 2–3 days acclimatization — build into prep if relevant
- **Rainy season (May–October):** Schedule exteriors in the dry morning hours; afternoon rain is frequent and unpredictable — always have interior cover set ready in rainy season
- **Holiday concentration:** Semana Santa (Holy Week), Día de Muertos (late Oct/early Nov), and the Christmas–Epiphany stretch affect crew availability and location access; schedule these windows carefully

## 2. Spain

### How the 1st AD Operates in Spain

Spain has a sophisticated production industry with two major TV hubs (Madrid and Barcelona) and a strong feature tradition. The 1st AD navigates Spanish union structures, language-specific logistics (Castilian, Catalan, Galician, Basque), and a production culture that values structured prep.

### Union Obligations Affecting the Schedule

**SETT (Sindicato Español de Técnicos de la Cinematografía y del Audiovisual)**
- Standard shooting day: 10 hrs working, not including 1 hr meal break (11 hrs total call block maximum before overtime)
- Overtime: Triggered after 10 working hours; rates escalate with each additional hour
- Turnaround: **Minimum 11 hrs** between wrap and next call — stricter than many jurisdictions
- Consecutive night shoots: Maximum **5 consecutive nights** before a mandatory day off is required
- Meals: Break required within 5 hrs of call; penalty for non-compliance varies by collective agreement

**Actors' Unions (FAAE / Sindicatos de Actores)**
- Principal cast rest: 12 hrs minimum between calls
- Sunday/holiday shooting: premium doubles in many agreements; avoid unless budgeted
- Dubbing artists: separate agreements — not a 1st AD concern unless scheduling voice sessions

### Child Actor Regulations (Spain)
- Children under 16: require written authorization from parents AND the Inspección de Trabajo
- Maximum on-set hours:
  - Under 7 years: 4 hrs/day
  - 7–16 years: 6 hrs/day
  - 14–16 years with special authorization: up to 8 hrs/day
- No night shooting for minors under 16 (night = after 21:00)
- Education tutor required if child misses school days

### Permit Requirements Affecting Schedule

| Permit Type | Lead Time | Impact on Schedule |
|-------------|-----------|-------------------|
| Local filming permits (Ayuntamiento) | 1–4 weeks (varies by city) | Madrid: 2–3 wks; Barcelona: 2–4 wks; rural: 1–2 wks |
| Port Authority (dock/coastal scenes) | 3–5 weeks | Maritime Authority approval separate from local permits |
| AESA drone operations | 2–3 weeks | Controlled airspace near airports requires advance filing |
| Heritage sites (Patrimonio Histórico) | 4–8 weeks | Any shoot in protected historical monument — start early |
| Road closures | 4–6 weeks | DGT (Dirección General de Tráfico) + local Ayuntamiento |
| Police/Guardia Civil cooperation for action sequences | 4–8 weeks | Essential for any vehicle chase or public-safety scene |
| Parque Nacional / Natural Space | 4–10 weeks | Protected nature areas require environmental impact statement |

### Practical Set Management in Spain

- **Siesta culture is gone in production** — Spanish film crews no longer observe midday breaks on set; call times are consistent with international norms
- **Regional languages:** Shooting in Catalonia with Catalan-speaking background artists, or in the Basque Country — coordinate extras casting with local agencies
- **Heat in Andalucía and Murcia (June–September):** Exterior heat can make afternoon shooting brutal; flip schedule to early AM and late PM; midday = prep/interior
- **Madrid at altitude (667m):** Lower oxygen than sea level — not significant for most crew, but note for endurance-intensive stunt days
- **Barcelona traffic grid:** Company moves within the eixample can take 30–45 min; avoid cross-city moves during morning (8–9 AM) and evening (18–19 PM) peaks
- **Canarias productions (Gran Canaria, Tenerife, Lanzarote):** Weather is consistent year-round (best exterior shooting in Spain); ferry logistics for equipment between islands adds 1–2 days transit; AEAT customs for international equipment

## 3. Colombia

### How the 1st AD Operates in Colombia

Colombia has become one of Latin America's most robust production hubs, with strong local crew depth in Bogotá, Cartagena, and Medellín. The 1st AD must navigate security protocols, altitude variables, and logistics complexity that make Colombian shoots operationally more demanding than equivalent Mexican or Spanish productions.

### Union / Labor Obligations Affecting the Schedule

Colombia does not have a single dominant guild system like STPC or SETT, but labor law (Código Sustantivo del Trabajo) applies to all workers and carries significant schedule implications:

- **Standard working day:** 8 hrs; overtime after 8 hrs at 125% for first 2 hrs, then 150%
- **Night work premium:** Work performed between 21:00 and 06:00 carries a 35% premium — this is statutory, not negotiable
- **Sunday/holiday work:** 75% premium on all wages for Sunday work; 100% for official holidays
- **Turnaround:** 12 hrs legally required between shifts; no grace period in Colombian labor law
- **Meal obligations:** Meals at employer expense are industry standard (not always statutory, but union in practice)

**CNC (Centro Narrativo Colombiano) and FilmColombia productions** may have additional obligations tied to incentive agreements; coordinate with production lawyer.

### Child Actor Regulations (Colombia)
- Minors under 15: require ICBF (Instituto Colombiano de Bienestar Familiar) authorization
- Maximum on set:
  - Under 12: 4 hrs/day
  - 12–15: 6 hrs/day
  - 15–18: 8 hrs/day (with restrictions on content type)
- No night work for minors under 18 without special authorization
- Parent or ICBF-approved guardian required on set at all times
- **Flag:** ICBF authorization takes 2–4 weeks; start immediately if any minor appears in the script

### Permit Requirements Affecting Schedule

| Permit Type | Lead Time | Impact on Schedule |
|-------------|-----------|-------------------|
| Bogotá IDT filming permits | 2–4 weeks | Alcaldía de Bogotá — standard for public space |
| Cartagena DAPARD + IDT | 2–3 weeks | Historic district (walled city) requires separate permit from the Centro Histórico authority |
| Medellín filming permits | 2–3 weeks | Alcaldía de Medellín |
| Drone operations (Aerocivil) | 3–4 weeks | All drone work requires Aerocivil registration and zone clearance |
| Road closures | 4–8 weeks | Ministerio de Transporte + local authority; complex in Bogotá |
| Police security escorts | 2–4 weeks | Required for high-value equipment transport in some regions |
| Armed forces cooperation | 6–10 weeks | Ministry of Defense; any scene depicting military activity |
| National Natural Parks (PNN) | 6–12 weeks | MADS authorization; environmental impact required |
| Weapons / replica firearms | 4–6 weeks | INDUMIL / Policía Nacional — same lead time as Mexico SEDENA |

### Security Protocol — Non-Negotiable 1st AD Responsibilities

Colombian productions require a security layer that is foreign to many international 1st ADs. This is not optional:

- **Security coordinator** is a full-time production position on any shoot leaving central Bogotá, Medellín, or Cartagena
- **Daily security briefing:** Before every location move to non-urban or remote areas; crew must know protocols
- **No-go zones:** Security coordinator defines restricted areas for each production week; 1st AD never overrides this even for a shot
- **Nighttime exterior exterior shoots outside city center:** Require security coordinator approval and private security escort minimum
- **Valuable equipment transport:** Armed escort required for camera trucks and grip trucks moving between cities or to remote locations — build this into the company move timeline (add 1–2 hrs)
- **Wrap time in remote locations:** Push for wrap before sunset; never strand crew in remote locations after dark

### Practical Set Management in Colombia

- **Bogotá altitude (2,640m):** This is significant. Crew arriving from sea level need 2–4 days acclimatization. Stunt performers need 3–5 days. Budget acclimatization days into prep for international crews.
- **Bogotá traffic:** Among the worst in Latin America. Allow 60–90 min for any company move across the city; 2–3 hrs during rush hours. Ciclovía (Sunday car-free Sundays) actually helps for Sunday shoots in certain zones.
- **Rainy seasons:** Two: March–May and September–November. Extreme in the Llanos and Pacific coast regions. Interior cover sets are mandatory.
- **Cartagena heat (year-round):** 30–35°C with high humidity. Exterior calls should push to early morning (5:30–7 AM golden hour) and late afternoon. Midday work (10 AM–3 PM) is productivity-limiting and a safety concern.
- **Medellín spring weather:** Consistently pleasant (22–26°C). The best weather for year-round exterior shooting in Colombia. Rainy afternoon (2–5 PM) is common — schedule accordingly.

## 4. Cross-Territory Shoots

When a production spans two or more territories (Mexico + Colombia, Spain + Morocco, etc.):

### Key 1st AD Responsibilities

1. **DOOD spans both territories:** Cast must have rest days built in for international travel (1 day transit minimum, 2 days if crossing time zones or altitude changes)
2. **Two permit timelines running simultaneously:** Start permits in both territories from Day 1 of prep
3. **Union compliance in both jurisdictions:** Don't assume one territory's rules apply in another; get local labor advisor
4. **Equipment carnets and customs:** Gear crossing borders requires ATA Carnet; build 3–5 days buffer for customs clearance; 1st AD should be aware of which shooting days depend on specific equipment arriving
5. **Daily call time synchronization:** If director is in Territory A while 2nd Unit is in Territory B, align wrap/call communications across time zones

### Security and Logistics Bridge
- Assign a local AD in each territory during overlapping prep/shoot periods
- Remote 1st AD should never be the sole schedule decision-maker when operating outside their home territory; use local expertise
