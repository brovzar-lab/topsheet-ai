## 1. Theatrical Feature Film

### Typical Parameters
| Parameter | Low-Budget | Mid-Tier | Premium |
|-----------|-----------|---------|---------|
| Script pages | 90–105 pp | 100–115 pp | 110–130 pp |
| Shooting days | 20–30 | 30–45 | 45–70+ |
| Pages/day target | 3.5–5 | 2.5–4 | 2–3.5 |
| Prep weeks | 4–6 | 6–10 | 10–16 |
| Wrap days | 3–5 | 5–7 | 7–14 |

### Feature Scheduling Principles
- **Story order is a myth:** Never schedule in script order. Schedule by location efficiency first.
- **End on the simplest scenes:** Reserve the last shooting days for coverage that can be done anywhere — inserts, pickups, close-ups that don't require full company.
- **Front-load complexity:** Big stunt, big VFX plate, large extras day — these happen in the first third of the schedule when the crew is freshest and the director hasn't yet exhausted their decision-making energy.
- **The golden hours:** Magic hour (30 min window) requires pre-positioned camera, action choreographed, actors ready. You do not discover magic hour — you plan for it.
- **Action sequences:** An action sequence of 2 script pages may take 3–5 full days. Break it out scene-by-scene and estimate separately.

### Feature AD Team
- 1st AD (full prep to wrap + DI/pickup days)
- 2nd AD (starts 2–3 weeks into prep)
- 2nd 2nd AD (starts 1 week before shooting)
- Set PAs: 2–4 depending on scale
- Additional Set PAs on large extras days

## 2. TV Drama (Network / Broadcast)

### Typical Parameters (per episode)
| Format | Pages | Shoot Days | Pages/Day |
|--------|-------|-----------|-----------|
| 45-min drama | 42–52 pp | 7–9 | 5–7 |
| 60-min drama | 52–62 pp | 8–11 | 5–7 |
| 90-min special | 80–90 pp | 14–18 | 5–6 |

### TV Drama Scheduling Principles
- **Episode overlap:** On a running series, Ep 2 1st AD starts prep while Ep 1 is still shooting. The schedule machinery is continuous.
- **Series bible continuity:** Standing sets are available all season — schedule those scenes flexibly. Practical locations book up fast.
- **Day 1 of episode:** Typically opens with a company-wide safety meeting + set introductions if new director.
- **Episodic directors:** May have less film time than the 1st AD. Be the institutional memory on set.
- **Network vs. cable:** Network slots create hard delivery deadlines. Schedule so picture lock is achievable 6–8 weeks post-wrap minimum.

### Block Shooting (common in TV)
Two directors alternate in blocks of 2 episodes. The 1st AD must coordinate:
- Director A shoots Ep 1 while Director B preps Ep 2
- Shared standing sets create scheduling conflicts
- Main cast may be pulled between units on the same day — flag this immediately, it's a SAG/ANDA violation risk

### Series AD Staffing
- Supervising Producer assigns 1st AD to each episode or block
- On a returning series: 1 permanent 1st AD for standing sets continuity; rotating freelance 2nd ADs
- Crowd days: add BG ADs at 1 per every 50–75 extras

## 3. Streaming Drama (SVOD — Netflix, HBO Max, Apple TV+, etc.)

### Typical Parameters (per episode)
| Format | Pages | Shoot Days | Pages/Day |
|--------|-------|-----------|---------|
| 30-min SVOD drama | 28–35 pp | 5–7 | 5–6 |
| 45-min SVOD drama | 42–55 pp | 8–12 | 4.5–6 |
| 60-min SVOD drama | 55–70 pp | 10–14 | 4–6 |
| Limited SVOD event | 60–80 pp | 12–18 | 4–5.5 |

### Streaming vs. Network Differences
| Factor | Network TV | Streaming |
|--------|-----------|----------|
| Production value expectation | High | Cinema-level |
| Pages/day pressure | 6–8 pp/day | 4–6 pp/day |
| Showrunner authority | High | Very high |
| Schedule flexibility | Low (broadcast windows) | Moderate (delivery-driven) |
| Delivery timeline | 8–10 wks post-wrap | 10–16 wks post-wrap |

**Key:** Streaming platforms treat every episode like a short film. The 1st AD must resist network-TV-speed pressure. A 45-min streaming drama episode at 8 pages/day will compromise production value.

### Streaming Series AD Staffing
- Full AD department from Day 1 (not a lean setup)
- 1st AD typically has final say on day calls; showrunner may override only in extreme cases
- Expect VFX notes on virtually every exterior — plan accordingly

## 4. Limited Series / Miniseries

### Typical Parameters
| Format | Total Pages | Total Shoot Days |
|--------|------------|-----------------|
| 4-episode limited (30 min each) | 100–140 pp | 20–35 days |
| 6-episode limited (45 min each) | 240–330 pp | 50–75 days |
| 8-episode limited (60 min each) | 400–480 pp | 80–120 days |

### Scheduling a Limited Series
- Treat the entire season as a single long feature, then divide into episode blocks
- Cross-episode grouping: if a practical location appears in episodes 2 and 6, shoot all its scenes together even if it means shooting out of episode order
- Cast DOOD spans the entire series — hold management becomes critical (a principal held across 90 shooting days is a massive cost center)
- Plan for director changes between episodes; built-in 1 to 1.5 days transition for each new director block

## 5. TV Comedy / Sitcom

### Typical Parameters
| Format | Pages | Shoot Days | Pages/Day |
|--------|-------|-----------|-----------|
| Single-cam 30 min | 28–38 pp | 4–6 | 6–8 |
| Single-cam 30 min (premium) | 28–35 pp | 5–7 | 5–6 |
| Multicam (studio) | 38–50 pp | 1–2 studio days | N/A — staged performance |
| Multicam table reads | N/A | 1 table read day | N/A |

### Comedy-Specific Notes
- **Multicam:** Shoot is a live performance with 3–4 cameras. The 1st AD manages audience and camera logistics. Written schedule is almost irrelevant — performance drives the day.
- **Single-cam comedy:** Faster than drama because dialogue is often static. But comedic timing may require many more takes per setup than drama.
- **Improv considerations:** If the director and cast work improvisationally, budget for longer coverage per scene. Track when the last good take was and call it.
- **Reaction shots:** Comedy relies heavily on reaction coverage. Budget time for full reaction coverage on every joke-heavy scene.

## 6. Pilot Episodes

### Why Pilots Are Unique
A pilot is simultaneously:
- A proof-of-concept for a potential series
- A production design bank for the entire subsequent season
- A character establishment document
- A financial audition for the network/platform

**Result:** Pilots take longer and cost more per page than any subsequent episode.

### Typical Parameters
| Format | Pages | Shoot Days | vs. Regular Episode |
|--------|-------|-----------|---------------------|
| 30-min comedy pilot | 32–40 pp | 6–10 | +30–50% days |
| 60-min drama pilot | 55–65 pp | 12–18 | +40–60% days |
| 90-min drama pilot | 80–95 pp | 16–24 | +50–70% days |

### Pilot Scheduling Principles
- **Design the standing sets first:** All standing sets (main character homes, offices, recurring locations) must be fully dressed and lit before first shooting day. This extends art department prep significantly.
- **Full coverage on every scene:** Network/platform execs will review every scene. Every setup needs full coverage — no shortcutting on the pilot.
- **Pickup tolerance is low:** If the pilot doesn't land, there's no season. Say this directly if the director is behind pace.
- **Casting is still in flux sometimes:** Be prepared for late cast changes during prep — update breakdown sheets immediately.

## 7. Second Unit

### When Second Unit Applies
- Action sequences requiring full stunt company without principal actors
- Background/establishing shots without principal cast
- Pick-up shots the main unit didn't complete
- Splinter unit (subset of main company for inserts/cutaways)

### Second Unit AD Staffing
- 2nd Unit 1st AD is fully autonomous — separate call sheet, separate schedule
- Reports to Main Unit 1st AD and Line Producer
- Must have stunt coordinator, safety officers, and own camera crew
- Must communicate daily pickup list to main unit at wrap

### Coordinating Two Units
- DOOD must reflect both units simultaneously — never double-book principal cast
- On days when a principal appears in both units: confirm union rest period between calls
- Budget: second unit is not "free" — it's a full crew day at reduced rate
