## 1. Labor & Union Framework

### Labor Framework
- Colombia operates under the Código Sustantivo del Trabajo (CST)
- No single dominant film union (unlike STPC in Mexico)
- Most crew engaged on contrato de prestación de servicios (service contract) or contrato de obra (project contract)
- ACFI (Asociación Colombiana de Filmación) lobbying for stronger industry frameworks
- **Key:** Prestaciones sociales are mandatory regardless of contract type when real employment relationship exists — courts routinely re-classify contracts

### Exchange Rate Context (2024)
- USD/COP: ~4,000–4,300 COP per USD (volatile; verify at budget time)
- EUR/COP: ~4,300–4,700 COP per EUR
- Most international co-productions price in USD; local vendors quote in COP

## 4. Tax Incentives

### FilmColombia (FDC — Fondo para el Desarrollo Cinematográfico)
- Cash rebate: 20% on qualifying Colombian spend for international co-productions
- Cash rebate: 40% for Colombian national productions
- Minimum spend: USD $1M for international productions
- Cultural requirement: Colombian creative participation required
- **Timeline:** Application before production starts; reimbursement typically 6–12 months post-delivery
- Administered by: Proimágenes Colombia + MinCultura

### CINA (Certificado de Incentivo Nacional Audiovisual)
- More recent mechanism under Ley del Cine reforms
- Tax certificate transferable to investors
- Ranges: 35–60% depending on production category and Colombian content

### ProColombia Services
- Location scouting support (free)
- Permit facilitation
- Introductions to local service companies
- Cannot substitute for local production services partner

## 5. City-by-City Logistics

### Bogotá (Primary hub)
- Elevation: 2,600m — altitude affects crew stamina and equipment performance. Budget acclimatization time for incoming international crew.
- Best for: period productions (Colonial/Republican architecture preserved), urban drama, interior studio work
- Studio infrastructure: Intermedio, Estudios Cuarzo, Colombia Audiovisual
- Traffic: Severely congested. Base camps and company trucks need strategic placement. Add 20% to inter-location move times.
- Weather: Bimodal rain seasons (April–May, October–November). Bogotá can have 4 seasons in a day. Budget weather days.

### Medellín
- Significantly warmer; 1,495m elevation
- Strong for: contemporary urban, narco-era period, fashion/commercial
- Smaller crew base than Bogotá; key HODs should be imported or booked early
- Infrastructure improving rapidly post-2015; city is highly production-friendly

### Cartagena
- Coastal, hot, humid; strong year-round sun
- Best for: Caribbean-set stories, colonial architecture, water work
- Smaller local crew base — most department HODs imported from Bogotá (+logistics cost)
- Hurricane season: June–November; weather days essential
- Equipment: moisture control essential for electronics

### Cali
- Hot Pacific-coast climate; 995m elevation
- Best for: salsa culture stories, Pacific coast narratives
- Limited production infrastructure — requires nearly full import of Bogotá-based crew
- Budget additional transport/logistics vs. Bogotá productions

## 6. Permit Requirements

| Requirement | Lead Time | Who Issues |
|-------------|-----------|-----------|
| National park filming | 6–10 weeks | Parques Nacionales Naturales |
| Bogotá public filming permit | 3–5 weeks | Alcaldía Mayor / Idartes |
| Cartagena historical center | 4–6 weeks | Instituto de Patrimonio y Cultura |
| Military/police presence | 4–6 weeks | Min. Defensa / Policía Nacional |
| Drone operations | 3–4 weeks | Aeronáutica Civil (AEROCIVIL) |
| Firearms on set | 6–8 weeks | Indumil + Policía — start immediately |
| Air travel/helicopter | 2–3 weeks | AEROCIVIL |

**Security note:** Productions in certain regions require security assessments. ProColombia or local production services companies can advise by location. Don't skip this step.

## 7. Common Budget Mistakes in Colombia

1. **Prestaciones at 25%** — Most first-time Colombia producers use Mexican or "Latin American average" fringes. The correct number is 47–52%. On a $500K USD below-the-line, this difference is $110K+.

2. **Not budgeting altitude acclimatization in Bogotá** — International crew arriving from sea level needs 2–3 days at minimum; action sequences or stunt work need 5+ days. Budget the days and any medical standby.

3. **Underestimating location complexity** — Colombia has extraordinary locations but access complexity is high. Permit timelines for national parks and historical sites regularly exceed 8 weeks. Early scouting and locking is essential.

4. **Currency volatility** — COP has moved 20–30% in single quarters. Lock exchange rate assumptions in contracts and set up a FX hedging position or contingency reserve.

5. **Underbudgeting production services company markup** — International productions coming in without a Colombian partner routinely face 15–25% service company markup on all local spend. This is legitimate but must be budgeted.

6. **Bogotá traffic** — Not budgeting extra move time or shooting days for company moves across Bogotá. A move that looks like 20 minutes on Google Maps can be 90 minutes during rush hour.

7. **Missing FilmColombia documentation** — The 20% rebate requires specific documentation chain from Day 1. Productions that bring in foreign vendors as full invoice recipients instead of routing through Colombian entities lose incentive eligibility.

8. **Security and fixers** — Underestimating the cost and importance of a local security fixer for locations outside Bogotá/Medellín/Cartagena centers. This is a real line item, not a soft cost.
